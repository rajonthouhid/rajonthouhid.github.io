## Current Research

### Development of 3D-Printed Porous Structures for Compost Aeration

I am conducting my master’s thesis research at Grand Valley State University under the supervision of Dr. Catherine Molloseau.

The research focuses on designing, fabricating, and evaluating 3D-printed porous aeration structures for laboratory-scale composting systems. The goal is to improve airflow distribution and study how aeration affects compost temperature, carbon dioxide concentration, relative humidity, and mass reduction.

## Research Objectives

The main objectives of this research are to:

- Design functional porous aeration structures
- Fabricate the structures using additive manufacturing
- Measure and control airflow supplied to compost samples
- Compare different aeration schedules
- Evaluate aerated and non-aerated composting conditions
- Monitor temperature, carbon dioxide, relative humidity, and mass
- Analyze airflow distribution using computational fluid dynamics
- Identify practical aeration strategies for laboratory-scale composting

## Experimental System

The experimental system includes six thermally isolated compartments. Each compartment contains a plastic container filled with a controlled compost mixture.

A 3D-printed PLA aeration plate is positioned at the bottom of each container. Air is supplied through tubing connected to the center of the plate and distributed upward through sixteen holes.

Each container contains approximately 1,200 grams of compost material.

The compost mixture includes:

- Orange peels
- Potatoes
- Carrots
- Cauliflower
- Coffee grounds
- Wood chips

## Aeration Conditions

Two experimental batches were conducted.

### Batch 1

- Containers A, B, and C: 10 minutes of aeration every 2 hours
- Containers D, E, and F: 10 minutes of aeration every 3 hours

### Batch 2

- Containers A, B, and C: 10 minutes of aeration every 60 minutes
- Containers D, E, and F: No aeration

The measured airflow rate was approximately **0.632 L/min per container**.

## Data Collection

The following parameters were monitored:

- Compost temperature
- Ambient temperature
- Carbon dioxide concentration
- Relative humidity
- Container mass
- Visual changes
- Experimental observations

A LabQuest 3 system was used for sensor-based data collection.

Measurements were collected for approximately 10 minutes per container. The testing order was alternated daily to reduce possible time-of-day bias.
